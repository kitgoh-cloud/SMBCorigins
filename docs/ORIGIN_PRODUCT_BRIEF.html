◆ Executive Summary

In one page.

The problem. Corporate banking onboarding for MNCs is the most painful phase of the client relationship — 90 to 180 days, hundreds of manual touches, repeated document submissions to different internal teams, and opaque status. RMs spend ~40% of their time on administrative work that could be automated. The bank loses revenue to slow time-to-live, and increasingly to digital-native competitors.

The opportunity. SMBC has publicly committed to onboarding and lifecycle transformation through the Fenergo rollout (Jan 2026) and launched SMBC Connect (Apr 2026) as the new global transaction banking portal. What's missing is the front door — the onboarding experience that feeds into SMBC Connect and demonstrates the future state of how clients begin their SMBC relationship.

The proposal. Build SMBC Origin — a dual-persona web prototype (client portal + RM cockpit) showcasing four agentic AI capabilities: entity structure intelligence, live document extraction, portfolio-aware RM copilot, and auto-drafted credit memos. Targets ~5 day TTL versus today's 90–180. Built as a ~7 day engagement using Next.js, Supabase, and Claude API.

The outcome. An internal capability showpiece and client workshop playback asset — demonstrating SMBC's ambition beyond what competitors (DBS IDEAL, HSBCnet, Standard Chartered Straight2Bank) have yet put into market.

01 · Strategic context

Why this, why now.

SMBC Origin is not built in a vacuum. It sits directly downstream of two initiatives SMBC has already committed to publicly, and directly upstream of one of the largest unaddressed pain points in corporate banking globally. The timing window is real.

1.1 · The market backdrop

Corporate banking has been the slowest segment of banking to digitise. Retail has been through two waves of transformation (digital-first, then mobile-first); SME banking is mid-wave with challengers like Airwallex, Wise Business, Revolut Business. Corporate and wholesale banking — where relationships are high-touch, high-value, and high-complexity — has lagged because the problems are harder and the cost of "almost right" is far higher.

That has changed in the last 18 months. Three forces have converged to open a real window:

  • Agentic AI moving from lab to production — capable of reasoning across multi-step workflows, not just classifying or extracting
  • Corporate treasurer expectations — the post-pandemic CFO expects consumer-grade enterprise tools and will move banks to get them
  • Regulatory tolerance for continuous (perpetual) KYC — enabling automation that was previously blocked by periodic-review compliance postures

1.2 · SMBC's stated direction

Two public commitments anchor this brief in SMBC's existing strategy:

Anchor 1 · January 2026

Fenergo rollout in Americas

SMBC Americas announced deployment of Fenergo as its client lifecycle management platform, describing it as part of a "multi-year transformation of uplifting our core capabilities, simplifying the technology infrastructure and removing manual processes from our workflow." This signals executive commitment to onboarding modernisation at infrastructure level.

Anchor 2 · April 2026

SMBC Connect launches in the US

New global transaction banking portal went live in the US market, with the stated design philosophy that "cash management systems should be as intuitive and frictionless as the apps on our smartphones." SMBC has committed to progressively rolling SMBC Connect out across North America and beyond, with transaction banking identified as a "key growth focus" in the Group's three-year strategic plan.

The gap

Both Fenergo and SMBC Connect target post-onboarding experiences. Neither yet addresses the pre-onboarding journey — the 3–6 month wait that remains the most visible point of friction. SMBC Origin demonstrates what this missing front door could look like.

  1. Strategic alignment. Japanese MNCs expanding into APAC and EMEA are SMBC's defining segment globally. Every major regional office is structured around Japanese corporates desks.
  2. Complexity is the showcase. MNC onboarding is the most complex case — multi-entity, multi-jurisdiction, multi-product. Solving for this segment demonstrates capability at the hardest end; simpler segments fall out naturally.
  3. Demonstrable cross-border value. The UBO intelligence agent and jurisdiction-aware document logic are most valuable precisely when the client operates across 4+ jurisdictions — the default for MNCs.

02 · The problem

What we're actually solving.

This is the section most Product Briefs skip or shallow. It matters. The reason corporate onboarding is slow is not simply "we haven't digitised yet" — it's a series of interlocking root causes that explain why digitisation alone hasn't fixed it.

Beyond time cost, the qualitative toll on RMs is significant. Senior RMs — expensive commercial talent — spend a material share of their week on activity that is effectively administrative. This is a major retention and morale issue.

2.4 · Bank pain, quantified

  • Revenue friction — delayed activation = delayed transaction flow, FX spread, and interest income. For a USD 50M facility, each week of delay is material.
  • Operational cost — industry estimates per-application onboarding cost at USD ~128 per account at scale. MNC accounts are materially higher given complexity.
  • Revenue loss from abandonment — industry reports £3.3B annual revenue loss across banks due to KYC/AML friction.
  • Inconsistent risk — manual screening disposition means inconsistent judgment across RMs and compliance officers
  • Audit overhead — every manual touch is an audit trail obligation

2.5 · Root causes (why digitisation alone hasn't fixed it)

This matters because prior waves of "digital onboarding" investments have largely failed to materially move MNC time-to-live.

  1. Siloed systems. Most banks have distinct systems for KYC, credit, product provisioning, and client management. Digitising each in isolation doesn't remove hand-offs between them.
  2. Compliance-first culture. Every shortcut requires compliance sign-off. Even when technology enables a faster path, organisational risk tolerance constrains it.
  3. Jurisdictional fragmentation. A single MNC spans 4+ jurisdictions; each has distinct documentation and regulatory requirements. Unifying UX across them is non-trivial.
  4. Client data not self-service-able. In many banks, clients cannot directly upload, check, or correct their own data — everything is mediated by RM or ops.
  5. No single source of truth. Entity data in KYC system; financial in credit; product in core banking. No single view means no single flow.
  6. AI deployed point-wise, not end-to-end. Banks have deployed AI for document extraction OR screening OR memo summarisation — never all at once across a single lifecycle.
Insight

The opportunity is not "digitise onboarding." That's been tried. The opportunity is to re-orchestrate the entire journey around a shared state + agentic AI, so every step builds on the previous automatically rather than requiring a human to push data from one system to the next.

03 · Vision & value proposition

What we're aiming for.

The RM sends one invite. AI unwraps the corporate structure, extracts data from every document, screens every entity, configures products across every jurisdiction, and drafts the credit memo. The client is live in days, not months — and their RM spent their time on the relationship, not paperwork.
— The 30-second pitch

3.1 · Design principles

  1. AI is quiet, not loud. Fresh Green is rationed. When you see it, intelligence happened. The cockpit is calm; the AI is a well-dressed colleague, not a chatbot that shouts.
  2. Parallel, not sequential. The RM and client operate in parallel lanes. AI bridges them. Nothing blocks waiting for a human button-push unless judgment is genuinely required.
  3. Explain the magic. Every AI decision is auditable — confidence scores, source registries, extraction logs, citations. The RM is in command, not overruled.
  4. Heritage through restraint. Japanese design sensibility — whitespace, precision, considered typography. Kanji as watermark, not decoration.
  5. Demo every moment. This is a showpiece. If an AI moment isn't visually memorable, it fails the mission.

06 · AI capability set

The four hero moments, fully specced.

Each AI capability is designed as a hero moment — a visibly memorable demonstration of what agentic AI enables. Below: detailed specs for each, including behaviour, confidence model, failure handling, UX specification, and data flow.

◆ Hero Moment 1

UBO Intelligence Agent

The signature moment. When James clicks "Run structure analysis" the UI animates the corporate tree building itself, layer by layer, with live narration. Each node pulses in as it resolves. Shell companies appear as dashed nodes. Natural persons appear at the top with confidence scores.

What it does
  • Queries jurisdictional corporate registries in parallel (ACRA, Companies House, CR-HK, Japan hōjin)
  • Builds entity hierarchy layer-by-layer
  • Unwraps shell companies (BVI, Cayman) recursively
  • Identifies natural persons at top of structure
  • Produces ownership narrative with confidence per node
Confidence model
  • 90–100% — confirmed via authoritative registry filing
  • 70–89% — inferred from multiple corroborating sources
  • 50–69% — inferred from single source; flag for RM review
  • <50% — insufficient data; escalate to human
Failure handling
  • Registry API failure → retry with exponential backoff; fall back to client-supplied data
  • Unreachable jurisdiction → flag node as "manual research required"
  • Conflicting data across sources → show both, flag for disposition
UX specification
  • React Flow for the tree; Framer Motion for node reveals staggered 120ms
  • Narration streams beside tree as plain text
  • Confidence shown as colour intensity on node border
  • Click any node → full provenance panel (source, timestamp, raw data)
◆ Hero Moment 2

Document Extraction Agent

Client drops a PDF. Right panel shows "Reading document..." with streaming progress. Middle panel shows live extraction log. Left panel populates structured fields one by one. Discrepancy badges appear if data conflicts with the entity tree from Stage 2.

What it does
  • OCR + LLM vision on uploaded PDFs (including Japanese)
  • Extracts structured fields per document type
  • Cross-references extractions against Stage 2 entity tree + registry data
  • Flags discrepancies with confidence + suggested resolution
  • Translates Japanese-language docs inline when needed
Document types supported
  • Certificate of Incorporation (all in-scope jurisdictions)
  • Memorandum & Articles (M&A)
  • Board resolutions
  • Signatory identification (passport, national ID)
  • Audited financial statements (for credit)
  • Japanese corporate registry extracts (登記簿)
Discrepancy handling
  • Extracted data ≠ Stage 2 tree → amber flag; client disposes or RM escalates
  • Conflicting data across 2+ documents → flag with both values
  • Confidence <70% on any field → field marked "needs review"
UX specification
  • Three-pane layout: PDF preview · extraction log · structured fields
  • Fields pulse in Fresh Green tint as they populate
  • Discrepancies show inline with orange border + "⚠" affordance
  • Accept / Edit / Request more info per field
◆ Hero Moment 3

RM Copilot

Persistent sidecar across the RM cockpit. ⌘J to summon or tap the Fresh Green CTA. Natural-language interface over the RM's entire portfolio. Reasons across applications, entities, documents, memos, screening. Real Claude API with tool-calling into Supabase.

Example prompts (demo)
  • "What's outstanding on Kaisei?"
  • "Draft a KYC memo summarising screening results for Kaisei"
  • "Compare Kaisei's risk profile against my portfolio average"
  • "Who else in my portfolio is expanding to Singapore? Any parallels?"
  • "Summarise this application in 3 bullets for my Monday call"
  • "What's my pipeline value at risk this week?"
  • "Show me applications stalled >48 hours"
Tool-calling capabilities
  • Read any data the RM has access to (role-scoped via RLS)
  • Generate text (memos, summaries, emails)
  • Propose draft actions for RM approval
  • Never autonomously mutates state without explicit RM confirm
Governance
  • All prompts + responses logged with timestamps
  • Model version pinned per session for auditability
  • Outputs flagged as "AI-generated" before insertion downstream
  • Confidence shown where meaningful
UX specification
  • Slide-in sidecar from right, 400px wide desktop, full-screen mobile
  • Conversation view with turn-by-turn history
  • Command bar at bottom; suggested prompts when idle
  • Structured outputs (memo, summary) can be "send to..." an application
◆ Hero Moment 4

Credit Memo Drafter

The highest-value AI moment from RM perspective. Click "Draft credit memo". Template appears. Each section streams in as AI writes it, with source pins linking back to data used. RM reviews section-by-section with Accept / Edit / Regenerate. Final memo exportable as PDF with full provenance appendix.

Memo structure (7 sections)
  1. Executive summary — 1 para, the ask, the recommendation
  2. Client overview — from entity tree + industry reference data
  3. Financial analysis — 3-year spread
  4. Proposed facility structure — size, tenor, purpose, drawdown
  5. Risk rating & key risks — from screening + industry + financials
  6. Covenants & conditions precedent — from policy library
  7. Recommendation — approve / approve w/ conditions / decline
Sources the drafter pulls from
  • Entity tree from Stage 2 (structure)
  • UBOs from Stage 2 (ownership, control)
  • Uploaded audited financials from Stage 3
  • Screening results from Stage 4 (risk)
  • Client's credit ask from Stage 5
  • SMBC policy library (mocked — covenants & conditions)
  • Industry reference data (mocked — sector outlook)
Quality controls
  • Every numerical claim has a source pin
  • Any inferred (vs. sourced) claim is flagged
  • RM can Accept, Edit, or Regenerate each section
  • Final memo requires RM sign-off before submission
UX specification
  • Full-page document layout; hover any statement to see source pin
  • Sections stream in with visible progress
  • Sidebar shows memo outline; click to jump
  • Export to PDF with full provenance appendix

6.5 · Supporting AI (not hero)

  • Screening Agent — runs sanctions/PEP/adverse media in parallel, clears obvious false positives autonomously, narrates amber/red hits with recommended disposition
  • Perpetual KYC watchtower (teaser) — post-activation, monitors registry filings, adverse media, sanctions updates; triggers alerts on change

6.6 · AI governance & trust

Every AI capability in Origin is designed with governance from day one. This matters for banking where AI deployment must survive compliance, audit, and regulatory review.

  • Full audit log — every prompt, response, tool call recorded
  • Confidence surfacing — every output comes with confidence indicator
  • Source provenance — every factual claim links to source data
  • Human-in-the-loop by default — AI proposes; humans approve anything material
  • Model version pinning — auditable which model produced which output
  • Override controls — RM / compliance can reject any AI disposition with reason

7.2 · Data model (summary)

10 Supabase tables, lean, all with RLS. Full schema in ORIGIN_DESIGN.md:

  • profiles — extends auth.users; role (rm / client_admin / client_user)
  • organizations — corporate customer parent
  • applications — one onboarding case; ties everything together
  • entities — legal entities under application (tree via self-reference)
  • ubos — natural persons with ownership / control
  • documents — uploads + extracted data as jsonb
  • screening_results — sanctions/PEP/media hits with AI narrative
  • products — requested products with config as jsonb
  • credit_memos — sections, status, sign-off
  • activities — unified timeline across client/RM/AI/system

7.3 · AI API architecture

AI calls are handled server-side via Next.js route handlers to keep keys secret and allow tool-calling into Supabase:

  • Streaming text (Credit Memo) — server streams Claude output via SSE
  • Tool-calling loop (RM Copilot) — multi-turn Claude with Supabase read tools
  • Scripted agentic (UBO, Doc Extract) — scripted sequences with mock data + realistic delays; production would orchestrate real registry APIs + Claude reasoning

7.4 · Security & data considerations

Security-aware patterns used throughout so architecture could survive a real deployment review:

  • Row-level security on every Supabase table
  • Role-scoped access — RMs see portfolio; clients see only their application
  • No PII in client logs — AI prompts sanitised before logging
  • Encryption at rest — Supabase default
  • Signed uploads — Supabase storage with signed URLs
  • Magic link auth — no password storage
  • Audit log immutability — activities table append-only via RLS
Prototype caveat

This is a showpiece, not production. Security hardening (pen testing, WAF, DLP, enterprise SSO) is deferred to productisation.

  • Master brief as source of truth — ORIGIN_DESIGN.md at repo root; Claude Code reads every session
  • Phased with clean gates — each phase has entry criteria + deliverables
  • Demo-driven development — walk through demo at each phase gate
  • Seed data early — Kaisei populated from Phase 1
  • Ship the vertical slice, then broaden — one complete journey before polishing any one stage

11.1 · Primary reactions we're targeting

Because this is a showpiece prototype, success is qualitative — measured by stakeholder reaction and downstream opportunity generation. After a 15-minute walkthrough, 3 of these 5 reactions landing = success:

  1. "That feels 5 years ahead of where we are today."
  2. "The UBO agent is magic."
  3. "I want the credit memo drafter yesterday."
  4. "The RM cockpit is a tool I'd actually use."
  5. "The client experience is consumer-grade."

  • Visual consistency — every screen feels part of one product
  • AI moment impact — each hero produces visible "wow" in internal testing
  • Seed data depth — Kaisei holds up to unscripted questioning

13.3 · Rough value case (if productised)

Directional, not commitment-grade:

  • TTL reduction — each day of TTL reduction = earlier revenue. USD 50M facility, each week earlier is material.
  • RM capacity released — if RMs reclaim 20% of week, at current desk size that's ≈ 5 RM FTEs across Japanese corporates desk globally
  • Operational cost per application — automation of doc extraction, screening, memo drafting could reduce ops cost 50–70%
  • Competitive win rate — consumer-grade onboarding as deal-differentiator
  • Downstream Connect adoption — clients onboarded through Origin have higher likelihood of deep Connect usage
Caveat

Value quantification is a full exercise for v0.2 / v1.0. Not appropriate to commit to numbers at prototype stage.

13.4 · Build vs. buy considerations

SMBC's existing Fenergo investment means onboarding/CLM infrastructure already exists. A natural productisation path positions Origin as the UX layer on top of Fenergo — Fenergo handles structured CLM data; Origin handles the AI-native client + RM experience. Preserves existing investment while adding the front-door layer Fenergo alone doesn't deliver.

Appendix A

Glossary.

Banking and AI terminology used throughout this brief.

  • ACRA
    Accounting and Corporate Regulatory Authority — Singapore's registrar of companies.
  • AML
    Anti-Money Laundering — regulatory and operational framework for detecting illicit funds.
  • BVI
    British Virgin Islands — offshore jurisdiction commonly used for holding structures.
  • CLM
    Client Lifecycle Management — process + systems that manage a client from onboarding through ongoing servicing.
  • COI
    Certificate of Incorporation — document proving legal formation of an entity.
  • Copilot
    AI assistant embedded in a user's workflow, providing natural-language interaction over their data.
  • E2E
    End-to-end — describing a journey from initiation to completion without gaps.
  • EDD
    Enhanced Due Diligence — deeper KYC performed for higher-risk clients.
  • FATCA / CRS
    Cross-border tax reporting frameworks — relevant to account opening documentation.
  • FATF
    Financial Action Task Force — global AML standards body.
  • Fenergo
    Enterprise CLM software used by large banks (including SMBC) to manage onboarding and ongoing KYC.
  • Hōjin
    Japanese corporate identification number (法人番号) — analogous to Singapore's UEN.
  • KYB
    Know Your Business — the corporate equivalent of KYC.
  • KYC
    Know Your Customer — mandatory identity and risk verification for banking customers.
  • LC / SBLC
    Letter of Credit / Standby Letter of Credit — trade finance instruments.
  • M&A
    Memorandum & Articles of Association — foundational corporate governance documents.
  • MNC
    Multinational corporation — clients with operations across multiple jurisdictions.
  • NPS
    Net Promoter Score — customer satisfaction metric.
  • PEP
    Politically Exposed Person — individual holding prominent public function, subject to enhanced screening.
  • pKYC
    Perpetual KYC — continuous monitoring of client risk rather than periodic manual review.
  • RACI
    Responsibility Assignment framework — Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed.
  • RLS
    Row-Level Security — database-level access control ensuring users see only authorised data.
  • RM
    Relationship Manager — the banker who owns and manages the client relationship.
  • SMBC Connect
    SMBC's global transaction banking portal, launched April 2026.
  • SSO
    Single Sign-On — enterprise identity pattern.
  • STP
    Straight-Through Processing — workflow completed end-to-end without human intervention.
  • TOM
    Target Operating Model — the future-state way of working an organisation is heading toward.
  • TTL
    Time-to-Live — elapsed time from application initiation to client going live.
  • UBO
    Ultimate Beneficial Owner — the natural person(s) who ultimately own or control a legal entity.
  • UEN
    Unique Entity Number — Singapore entity identifier.
  • WCAG
    Web Content Accessibility Guidelines — accessibility standard.
  • Wolfsberg
    Wolfsberg Group — consortium of 13 global banks issuing anti-financial-crime standards.
